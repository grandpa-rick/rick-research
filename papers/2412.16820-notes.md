# arXiv:2412.16820 — Weyl Alternation Sets are Order Ideals in Weak Bruhat Order

**Authors:** Anderson, Banaian, Ferreri, Goff, Hadaway, Harris, Harry, Mayers, Wang, Wilson
**Title:** "The support of Kostant's weight multiplicity formula is an order ideal in the weak Bruhat order"
**Version read:** v2 (14 Aug 2025)

---

## 1. Definitions (verbatim or near-verbatim)

### 1.1 Weyl alternation set (their version of "Kostant alternation set")

> **Definition 1.1.** For λ and µ integral weights of g, the Weyl alternation set is
>
>   A(λ, µ) = { σ ∈ W : ℘(σ(λ + ρ) − µ − ρ) > 0 },
>
> where ℘ is Kostant's partition function. Equivalently, σ ∈ A(λ,µ) iff σ(λ+ρ) − µ − ρ can be written as a nonnegative integer combination of positive roots.

**Indexing:** Elements of A(λ,µ) are *Weyl-group elements* σ ∈ W. Not subsets of positive roots; not Kostant partitions. The set is simply the support (= nontrivially-contributing terms) of Kostant's alternating sum for m(λ,µ).

### 1.2 Weak Bruhat orders

> **Definition 2.1.** Right weak (Bruhat) order (W, ≤_R) is the transitive closure of σ ⋖_R σ s_i whenever ℓ(σ s_i) > ℓ(σ). Left weak (Bruhat) order (W, ≤_L) is similarly defined by σ ⋖_L s_i σ whenever ℓ(s_i σ) > ℓ(σ).

Equivalently, σ <_R τ iff a reduced expression of σ is a *prefix* of some reduced expression of τ (and σ <_L τ iff suffix).

### 1.3 Order ideal

Standard: I ⊆ P is an order ideal iff y ∈ I and x ≤ y ⇒ x ∈ I. (They cite Stanley EC1.)

### 1.4 Influence / extended influence / pairwise independence (their auxiliary structure)

> **Definition 3.4.** For σ ∈ W, the **influence** is I(σ) = {i : s_i appears in a reduced word for σ} (well-defined by Lemma 3.8(1) = Matsumoto). The **extended influence** Ī(σ) is I(σ) together with all Dynkin neighbors of indices in I(σ). I(σ) is *connected* if it induces a connected subgraph of the Dynkin diagram.

> **Definition 3.5.** σ, τ ∈ W are **independent** if I(σ) ∩ Ī(τ) = ∅. A subset S ⊆ W is **pairwise independent** if every distinct pair is independent.

### 1.5 Basic allowable subwords

> **Definition 3.9.** For dominant integral λ and weight µ with A(λ,µ) ≠ ∅, BAS(λ,µ) is the unique subset S ⊆ A(λ,µ) with 1 ∉ S, every b ∈ S has connected influence, and every σ ∈ A(λ,µ) is a product of a pairwise independent subset of S.

## 2. Theorems (verbatim) + commentary

### Theorem 3.1 (main structural)

> Let λ be a dominant integral weight of a simple Lie algebra g with Weyl group W. Then for any weight µ, A(λ,µ) is a (possibly empty) order ideal in **both** the left and right weak Bruhat orders of W.

**Rick-style commentary.** The proof is one paragraph: if τ = σ s_i with ℓ(τ) > ℓ(σ), then σ(λ+ρ) − τ(λ+ρ) = 2(λ+ρ,α_i)/(α_i,α_i) · σ(α_i), which is a nonneg-integer multiple of a positive root because λ dominant integral forces the coefficient ≥ 0 and σ(α_i) ∈ Φ⁺ since the cover lengthens. Symmetric argument on the left. **Type-uniform** — works for every simple g.

### Corollary 3.2 (consecutive subword closure)

> If a reduced word of σ is a *consecutive* subword of a reduced word of some τ ∈ A(λ,µ), then σ ∈ A(λ,µ).

**Commentary.** Stronger than just "subword" — uses both left and right weak Bruhat ideal structure. This is the lever they use to forbid words.

### Theorem 3.10 (independence-set decomposition)

> Let λ dominant integral, A(λ,µ) ≠ ∅. (1) There is a unique S = BAS(λ,µ) ⊆ A(λ,µ) (1 ∉ S) of connected-influence elements such that every σ ∈ A(λ,µ) is a product of a pairwise independent subset of S. (2) Pairwise independent subsets of BAS(λ,µ) are in bijection with elements of A(λ,µ).

**Commentary.** This is a *simplicial-complex* / independence-system structure on A(λ,µ). It's also type-uniform but the elements of BAS depend on λ, µ. They give type-A_4, A_8, C_5, D_5 examples.

### Proposition 3.15 (sufficient conditions for BAS)

If S satisfies (C1) every simple s_i ∈ A(λ,µ) is in S, (C2) each element of S has connected influence, and (C3) any product στ of nonindependent S-pair either lies in S, factors as a shorter pairwise-independent product, or contains a consecutive subword not in A(λ,µ) — then S = BAS(λ,µ).

### Theorem 4.7 + Section 4–5: Type-A specialization

> In type A_r, BAS(α̃, −α̃) is precisely the list (a)–(e) of Proposition 4.3: simple reflections s_k, length-2 words s_{k+1}s_k and s_ks_{k+1} (interior), s_ks_{k+1}s_k, and s_{k+2}s_ks_{k+1}.

Plus full characterization of A_r(α̃, −α_{i,j}) for all negative roots and a Fibonacci-type recurrence (Prop 5.5) and a generating function (Theorem 5.14).

**Commentary.** Past Section 3 the paper is **type A only**.

## 3. How the order-ideal structure is USED

The order-ideal property (Theorem 3.1) is used in three downstream ways:

1. **Forbidden-subword pruning.** Cor. 3.2 says: if a small word w is *not* in A(λ,µ), then any τ containing w as a consecutive subword is not in A(λ,µ). In Section 4 they identify finitely many forbidden length-2/length-3/length-4 patterns (Lemmas 4.4, 4.5) and use Cor. 3.2 to rule out all bad τ at once.

2. **BAS uniqueness/existence (Theorem 3.10).** Existence of BAS uses the right-weak-order *cover decreasing* iteration: any σ ∈ A(λ,µ) factors as σ = b σ' with b ∈ S and σ' independent of b; the iteration terminates because b₁ <_R σ strictly decreases length.

3. **No sign-reversing involution.** This paper does **not** construct an involution on A(λ,µ). It does not pair Weyl elements to cancel (−1)^ℓ(σ) terms. The order-ideal result tells you *which* σ contribute, but not how they cancel. The q-analog computation (Section 6) is left to future work, and even Harry's conjecture is only proved in this paper for µ = −α_i (single simple root).

## 4. Priority-order question (the core question for Aug~)

**Does the paper provide a way to PRIORITIZE Weyl elements such that for each w, exactly one "move" applies first?**

**No.** The paper is purely a *structural / set-theoretic* statement about A(λ,µ):

- A(λ,µ) is an order ideal (Thm 3.1) — but no canonical maximal-element/cover decomposition is given. An order ideal generically has many incomparable maxima (e.g., Example 2.3 has two).
- BAS(λ,µ) (Thm 3.10) gives a *unique decomposition* of each σ as a pairwise-independent product, but those factors all *commute* (independence ⇒ commute), so there is no built-in priority among them — any order works to recover σ.
- The "first cover" of σ in (W, ≤_R) is not unique: descents D_R(σ) typically has size > 1.

What the paper *does* give that is canonical:

- A decreasing-induction skeleton on (W, ≤_R) with respect to *length*. This is a partial-order well-foundedness argument, not a total priority.
- The pairwise-independence simplicial structure (BAS-faces) — type-uniform but again unordered within the face.

There is **no coordinate-free total order on simple-reflection moves**, no priority among "long-long swaps" vs "simple reflections," and *no notion of subtype-shift moves* anywhere in the paper. The paper's setting is W-elements as sums in m(λ,µ), not pairs (w,π) with sign-reversing involutions on Kostant partitions π.

## 6. What this gives Aug~

**Verdict: this paper does NOT provide a coordinate-free / type-uniform priority order on the move set for Aug~.**

What it *does* give Rick that is potentially useful:

- A clean type-uniform fact: the support A(λ,µ) is a left-and-right weak-Bruhat order ideal. If Aug~ moves are required to *preserve A(λ,µ)*, weak-Bruhat covers furnish a canonical reduction direction (decrease length by one cover at a time).
- A *type-uniform* simplicial complex on A(λ,µ) via BAS (Thm 3.10). One could imagine indexing Aug~ orbits by BAS-faces, but the elements within a face commute → no intrinsic priority among them.
- A consecutive-subword closure (Cor 3.2): can be used to "forbid bad moves" globally rather than case-by-case.

What is **missing for B_n Aug~**:

- Any pairing of (w, π)-data: this paper only tracks w, never the Kostant partition π.
- Any notion of "long-long swap" / shifted-index move. The paper's elementary moves are simple reflections only (since it works with weak Bruhat covers).
- A *total* priority order on (simple reflection, sub-type) pairs. The paper has at best the partial well-founded order (W, ℓ).
- Any argument valid past type A. The structural Theorem 3.1 is type-uniform but everything downstream (BAS characterization, recurrences) specializes to A.

**Possible adaptation for Rick:** if one could lift Aug~ to act on A(λ,µ) (the support side), Theorem 3.1 says any sign-reversing pairing must respect the order-ideal structure — i.e., the Aug~ partner of a maximal-length σ must be at lower length. This is a *necessary condition* on Aug~ that the BGG/Verma side already satisfies, but it falls short of being a *priority recipe*. To get a priority, Rick would still need to pick, among the multiple right-descents D_R(σ) of σ, *which* simple reflection to apply — and the paper offers no canonical choice.

In short: this paper provides the *poset shape* of where Aug~ has to live, not the *flow* on it.
